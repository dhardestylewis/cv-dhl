# Climatebase Fellowship — Spoken Interview Script

Deadline: 02/24/2026 · Cohort 9: March 16 – June 5 · ~15 min total, both Qs in one sitting

## Q1: "Please tell us about your background"

Hi, I'm Daniel Hardesty Lewis. I'm finishing my Master's in Urban Planning at Columbia, and before that I spent five years at the Texas Advanced Computing Center working on flood and climate modeling. The thing I keep coming back to is a map I built — a one-meter resolution flood map generated from National Water Model outputs for Texas Emergency Management. When a hurricane hits, that's what first responders pull up to decide where to send resources. I built the flood maps for their Real-Time Inundation Mapping system.

That was part of a $40 million program called TDIS — the Texas Disaster Information System — where I was the primary technical point of contact between the engineering team, Texas Emergency Management, and first-responder agencies. I was running 800,000-node distributed jobs on leadership-class supercomputers to drive the climate and flood models behind it, managing over a million dollars in compute allocations across NOAA, the Army Corps, and other federal and state agencies.

One of the more interesting parts of that work was a direct partnership with the Army Corps of Engineers to model compound flood hazards along the Texas coast — the scenario where storm surge, river flooding, and heavy rainfall all hit at the same time. That compound scenario is what causes the worst outcomes, and it's poorly understood. We were integrating sub-meter topographic data with hydrological models to figure out how those hazards interact.

That experience is what radicalized me. I watched climate data either reach or completely fail to reach the people who needed it for life-or-death decisions. That's what made me want to build climate tools — not just run models in a computing center.

Before the senior role, I spent a year on DARPA's World Modelers program building disaster and food-security models for East Africa, working under Yolanda Gil — the president of AAAI. That's where I first published on tying climate, hydrology, and socioeconomic systems together into a single platform.

Out of the TDIS work, I founded Summit Geospatial to commercialize the elevation data layer I'd built. It's a statewide Texas DEM stitched from over 70 LiDAR datasets — accurate enough to tell you which side of the street floods. I built the whole web platform end-to-end. It's the kind of foundational infrastructure that every flood modeling and climate adaptation workflow in the state depends on.

I also volunteered for the Atlantic search and rescue of the Cheeki Rafiki. High-stakes decisions under uncertainty aren't just my job, they're something I choose to do.

## Q2: "What about the Fellowship inspired you to apply, and what are you hoping to gain from the experience and community?"

What drew me to Climatebase is honestly the gap in my own experience. I know flood modeling, disaster systems, and geospatial infrastructure deeply. But climate is much bigger than that. Energy, transportation, food systems, carbon removal, the circular economy — I don't have depth in those sectors, and I need it to figure out where my technical skills create the most leverage across the full landscape of climate solutions, not just the corner I already know. That's why I'm at Columbia studying Urban Planning — to understand the policy and land-use side of climate adaptation — and why I've been reading deeply into energy transition and carbon removal on my own. The fellowship's twelve-week curriculum with weekly expert-led sessions and over a hundred climate leaders per cohort is how I want to build the rest of that breadth, fast.

The other thing is community. I've been doing this work mostly as a founder and technical lead, which can be pretty isolating. Climate problems cut across so many domains at once — energy, policy, finance, land use, infrastructure — and no one person covers all of that. Climatebase brings together people from completely different backgrounds who are all pointed at the same problem. The sub-communities, the alumni network, the regional meetups — I'm in New York, and I know there's a strong presence here — that kind of cross-pollination is something I can't build on my own.

And the capstone project structure matters to me. I'm a builder. I ship production systems. I want to be around other people who are actively launching things and making real progress, not just studying the space.

What I'm hoping to walk away with is a real understanding of the full climate landscape, a network of co-founders and collaborators who are already deep in the space, and honestly a permanent shift in my trajectory. I've spent eight years building tools around climate data. Summit Geospatial is already a climate company — the elevation data powers flood modeling and adaptation planning across Texas. I want the fellowship to be what helps me scale that, and aim everything I build directly at climate outcomes going forward.

On the contribution side — I've taught graduate courses in machine learning and high-performance computing, and I've mentored engineers at every level. If a fellow needs help scoping a prototype, designing a data pipeline, or debugging something technical, that's where I show up.

- "That experience is what radicalized me" — keep or soften to "that changed everything for me" depending on comfort.
- If running long, cut Cheeki Rafiki and the USACE compound-flood paragraph first.
- If running short, add a beat about what it was like seeing your maps used during a real hurricane.
